  - [12:50 - 12:53] **Introducing AI Snake Oil**

    We generally categorize these tools based on what they do. We have Generative AI, creating content. We have Predictive AI, which someone in the poll mentioned; tools that attempt to forecast human behaviors and social outcomes like employment, educational achievements, public benefits, health risks for insurance, and so on. Up until ChatGPT became a massive wake-up call, the promise and brand of AI was largely focused on this predictive aspect. But the reading introduces this concept of "AI Snake Oil."

    As you can see from the picture on the slide, it refers to AI products that are actually fraudulent and reliable, meaning they definitely do not work as they have been promoted. The term was inspired by literal snake oil salesmen from a hundred years ago. They sold this liniment as a curable magic potion for every kind of health problem—rheumatism, toothaches, frostbite, you name it. Which, obviously, we all know in medical science is impossible. Different symptoms require different treatments. But these days, companies are selling AI algorithms that claim to solve all problems, regardless of the context, regardless of the industry. That is why they call it AI Snake Oil. There are so many examples of how these AI tools actually fail, even though they are heavily automated and aggressively marketed. Yes, they will claim to be efficient in the testing phase and claim to have a better prediction rate, but I can show you some real-world examples of where they fall apart.

    我们通常根据这些工具的功能对它们进行分类。我们有生成式人工智能（Generative AI），负责创建内容。我们有预测性人工智能（Predictive AI），投票中也有人提到了；这些工具试图预测人类行为和社会结果，如就业、教育成就、公共福利、保险的健康风险等等。在 ChatGPT 敲响巨大的警钟之前，人工智能的承诺和品牌在很大程度上集中在这种预测能力上。但阅读材料引入了“人工智能万灵油（AI Snake Oil）”这个概念。正如你在幻灯片上的图片所见，它指的是那些实际上具有欺骗性且不可靠的人工智能产品，这意味着它们绝对没有达到它们所宣传的效果。这个词的灵感来源于一百年前真正的卖蛇油的江湖骗子。他们把这种搽剂当作能包治百病的灵丹妙药来卖——风湿病、牙痛、冻伤，应有尽有。显然，我们在医学科学中都知道这是不可能的。不同的症状需要不同的治疗方法。但如今，公司正在销售声称能解决所有问题的人工智能算法，无论在什么语境下，无论在什么行业。这就是为什么他们称之为“人工智能万灵油”。尽管这些人工智能工具高度自动化且被积极推销，但它们实际上失败的例子不胜枚举。是的，他们会声称在测试阶段效率很高，并声称有更好的预测率，但我可以给你们看一些它们在现实世界中彻底崩溃的例子。

  - [12:53 - 12:56] **Failures in Predictive AI Systems**

    One catastrophic example was during the Coronavirus pandemic in the UK. Because students couldn't take their physical A-level exams, an algorithm was used to predict their final results based on their past performance and the historical performance of their schools. Now, at the time, the government genuinely thought it was a good idea, as taking physical tests was not an option. But it resulted in a fiasco where students from historically poorer schools were systematically downgraded, while those from elite schools were artificially boosted. The algorithm graded their postcode, not their ability. Another severe example happened recently, between 2019 and 2020, with the Dutch government. They faced collapse over a child benefits scandal. The Dutch tax authority used a predictive algorithm that wrongly claimed tens of thousands of families were claiming fraudulent childcare benefits. They pursued these parents, pushing them into financial ruin, forcing them to pay back money they legitimately deserved. A year later, a parliamentary inquiry found that these accusations were completely flawed for many reasons. To simply call it a "claim of damage" is too weak; people were pushed into financial crises, severe psychological distress, and families were torn apart. These are not small glitches. Why does this happen? We all know Generative AI like ChatGPT produces hallucinations, spitting out wrong information even if you explicitly tell it your policy. Whenever you ask it, "Why did you do that?", it just says, "Oh, I did it, I'm sorry." But this flaw applies to both generative AI and predictive AI. There are many reasons why these predictions fail, largely due to embedded biases.

  - [13:19 - 13:29] **The Six Reasons for the Claims-Reality Gap**

    There are many possible answers to why this gap persists, including the excellent points you've raised. I jotted down a few structural reasons based on Narayanan and Kapoor's arguments. First: incentives reward overclaiming. We are living in a capitalist world, and money is often the driving factor. Vendors and start-ups need attention, funding, and rapid adoption, so marketing presents the AI as a general-purpose tool that is completely ready for deployment. Second: AI capability is easy to demo, but very hard to evaluate in context. This is a massive reason why the gap isn't just because people are stupid or gullible. People see a polished demo and believe it. But a system can score well on a test yet be totally unreliable, unsafe, or unhelpful in everyday use. An AI is a sociotechnical system. To use the criminal justice algorithm as an example: developers building the AI software don't have access to the messy, real-world data sets that local police use daily. They test it in controlled settings. When they deploy it, the AI's performance degrades because we don't know how human workers flexibly or innovatively use (or misuse) these technologies. Third: ambiguous problem definitions, which I call "goalpost shifting." When an AI fails to deliver, the creators just change the definition of what success looks like. Fourth: reality shifts and data lag. Models learn from past data, but the world changes. A serious issue happened between 2022 and 2023 when the Russia-Ukraine war disrupted global data patterns, causing predictive models trained on peaceful periods to fail spectacularly. Fifth: hidden labor and infrastructure. Think of Siri, Alexa, or social media content moderation. We often completely forget about the human labor involved. Specifically, the cheap labor in countries across Africa and Southeast Asia. There are thousands of people working in sweatshop conditions to annotate, label, and classify data. They are the ones tagging an image as ethical or violent so the AI can learn. We don't see this hidden human foundation; we just see the "magic" of the AI. Sixth: accountability is often weak. When systems fail, responsibility is diffuse. The vendor blames the deployer, the deployer blames the frontline user, and harms are treated as unavoidable "errors." Based on all of this, my conceptualization of AI is that it is not a technically precise label. AI is an aspirational, sometimes fantastical project that encompasses an expanding collection of data-centric technologies. We are not tearing it down because we hate it; we are deconstructing it because we want to understand what AI really means. What it can do, what it cannot do, and what it *should* not do. We need to analyze it not just as a technology, but socio-culturally, politically, and economically.   - [13:43 - 13:49] **AI as Media and Social Transformation**

    Welcome back. Before the break, we were discussing the functional aspects of the change in communication. Now, people are seeing how AI may be fundamentally affecting the way we interact and communicate. If communication has historically been defined by what it means to be human, what happens when machines move in to communicate? AI isn't just a tool we communicate *through*; it is an entity we communicate *with*. This shifts the theoretical foundation from just Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) to Human-Machine Communication (HMC). But the reason our course title includes "Social Transformations" is because communication is about much more than what happens simply between individuals. It immediately raises a much bigger macro-level question: why do humans, institutions, and organizations communicate in the first place? Yes, we want to express ourselves, and we want to connect with one another socially, emotionally, and politically. But at the same time, communication is how society coordinates itself. It is how institutions make decisions. It is how rules are enforced, how societal value is assigned, how uncertainty is managed, and how legitimacy is produced—for example, through the media or political speeches. Communication is the mechanism for societal coordination, not just individual heuristic exchange. So, when AI steps into the role of communicator, it reshapes communication in ways that have stakes which are not just individual or interpersonal, but deeply institutional and structural.

    There is actually a great, classic book on this. It's a bit older and has nothing explicitly to do with AI, which is why I didn't put it on the reading list, but I highly recommend it. It is called *The Control Revolution*, written in 1986 by the American sociologist James Beniger. He analyzed media, communication, and information technologies as systems built fundamentally to maintain coordination and control as societies scaled up and became vastly more complex. For example, he looked at the 18th and 19th-century scientific revolutions—steam power and the railways. He argued that while these innovations were amazing, they caused a profound "crisis of control." Societies had to develop new means to address this crisis to continue coordinating. It wasn't just about the government oppressively controlling people; it was about how a rapidly growing society manages logistics. To handle the speed of railways and industrial production, societies developed the telegraph, bureaucratic management, and standardized time zones just to maintain control of information flows across complex networks. If you are interested in how technological innovation mandates the measurement and control of information, I highly recommend this book. If we think about AI from this perspective, why is AI being developed? We will discuss this in Week 3 when we cover political economy. Some people argue we are developing AI to reach AGI—the ultimate dream for people like Ray Kurzweil. But at the same time, AI is simply another mechanism for coordinating and controlling society. It is a new way of managing massive scales of data, restructuring how society is organized. When a communication technology this powerful is introduced, it will obviously have massive macro-level societal implications. This is why we call this course "AI, Media, and Social Transformations." We aren't just talking about AI in the media industry; we are looking at AI as a medium of communication that drives huge socio-economic changes, just like the telegraph and modern bureaucracy did in the past.

## Course syllabus
  - **Course Description** This interdisciplinary master’s course interrogates artificial intelligence not merely as technology but as an institutionalised medium that channels data, resources, and power, reshaping everyday life. Drawing on media and communications studies, sociology, political economy, critical AI studies, and Science and Technology Studies, it explores: (1) the historical and geographical trajectories of AI development; (2) AI’s integration into work, labour, creativity and social norms; and (3) legal-ethical debates on intellectual property, privacy, sustainability, disinformation and algorithmic bias across race, gender and class. Through critical readings, case studies and a research project, students analyse AI’s local and global impacts and debate future directions. The course is non-technical (no coding required), aimed at students seeking nuanced insight into AI’s role in reorganising economy, culture and governance.
  - **Learning outcomes**
    - Students will develop a contextual understanding of AI, its history, and evolution, to have an informed opinion for its future use and trajectory.
    - Students will understand the basics of the technologies behind AI, such as machine learning, deep learning, natural language processing, neural networks, and algorithms.
    - Students will be able to critically analyse AI as a form of media that structures and mediates data, information, social interactions, and work practices, and understand its broader societal implications.
    - Students will be able to examine the impact of AI on media and cultural industries, such as journalism, advertising, entertainment, and cultural production, considering AI-driven automation and personalisation.
    - Students will be able to evaluate the ethical, social, and regulatory challenges surrounding AI, such as algorithmic bias, disinformation, surveillance, intellectual property rights, and data governance.

## Assessment components
  20%	 
  Essays

  25%	AI Reflective Essay: Over 7 consecutive days, students will write a diary that records how their chosen AI system mediates the information access, social interactions, daily routines, work practices, or other aspects of everyday life. They will do so by documenting and analysing their personal interaction with one specific AI mediated system (e.g. a social media recommendation system, generative AI tool, virtual assistant, targeted advertising system, AI translation, photo enhancement app, or facial recognition). Students will also reflect on the wider social, ethical, and political implications of your observations.
  Project/Group Project

  35%	In groups, students will focus on a specific application of AI, e.g. inequalities in the AI and algorithmic era; mapping AI labour chains; AI deepfakes and democratic erosion; AI and creative or cultural production; AI and surveillance; AI and sustainability; AI futures and policy visions. The output will be a critical explanatory report (3,000 words) plus one creative piece (e.g. a short video clip, a podcast episode, digital storytelling, an infographic or data visualisation, a fictional policy or corporate brochure, a performance, or an interactive webpage) designed to communicate the gist of the report in a publicly intelligible way. Students are expected to address both the technical possibilities of the chosen AI application and its impacts on local society, and to discuss any relevant controversies as well as ethical and or legal issues.
